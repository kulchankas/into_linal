Block 1

Metric spaces, norms & topology

📖 Read pp. 11–23 — Metric spaces · Completeness · Compactness; norms with continuous linear maps pp. 101–106.
🤖 In ML today

Distance is the whole game for $k$-NN, $k$-means, DBSCAN and hierarchical clustering — the choice of metric (Euclidean, cosine, Mahalanobis) defines what "similar" means. Norms are how we regularize: the $L^1$ norm (lasso) drives sparsity, the $L^2$ norm (ridge / weight decay) controls magnitude. Embeddings (word, sentence, image vectors) are points in a metric space where cosine distance encodes meaning. Completeness/compactness are the abstract guarantee that "a best model exists" on a closed, bounded parameter set.

🔮 Next

Metric learning — learning the distance itself via contrastive / triplet losses (Siamese networks, CLIP) — and non-Euclidean embeddings (hyperbolic space for hierarchies, Riemannian/geometric deep learning). At industrial scale, vector databases + RAG are nearest-neighbour search in a metric space.

Block 2

Continuity, openness & connectedness

📖 Read pp. 24–38 — Continuity in metric spaces (connectedness pp. 27–36).
🤖 In ML today

Continuity = stability: a small input change should cause a small output change. Lipschitz continuity bounds how much a network can amplify a perturbation — the core quantity in adversarial robustness and many generalization bounds. Connected components of a classifier's level sets are literally the regions it groups together, and continuity of the loss is what makes gradient descent meaningful at all.

🔮 Next

Certified robustness (provable Lipschitz bounds, randomized smoothing), architectures with built-in 1-Lipschitz layers, and topological data analysis (persistent homology) that summarizes the shape and connectedness of data.

Block 3

Curves & arc length

📖 Read pp. 135–139 — rectifiable curves (lightly covered; mostly problem-set material).
🤖 In ML today

Training is a curve in parameter space — learning-rate schedules trace different paths to the same basin. Curve length on a data manifold is the basis of manifold learning (Isomap uses geodesic, not straight-line, distance), and latent-space interpolation in VAEs/GANs/diffusion is a curve whose smoothness determines sample quality.

🔮 Next

Neural ODEs and continuous-depth models define the network itself as a curve solving an ODE; flow matching & diffusion transport probability mass along curves; Riemannian optimization follows geodesics on constraint manifolds.

Block 4

Differentiability, gradient & the affine-approximation pipeline

📖 Read pp. 61–70 (1-D), 125–134 (Fréchet derivative), 140–150 (gradient & Jacobian in coordinates).
🤖 In ML today

This is the engine of deep learning. Backpropagation is the chain rule for Fréchet/Jacobian derivatives; the gradient is the descent direction; the first-order Taylor (affine) approximation is exactly what one SGD step trusts. Autodiff (PyTorch, JAX) is industrial-scale Jacobian computation. Jacobians also drive normalizing flows (change-of-variables) and sensitivity analysis.

🔮 Next

Differentiable everything — differentiable physics, rendering and simulators; implicit differentiation through optimization layers; JAX-style composable transforms (grad, jvp, vjp) as the substrate of scientific ML.

Block 5

Taylor's formula, the Hessian & classifying extrema

📖 Read pp. 71–77 (1-D extrema), 89–96 (Taylor), 151–158 (multivariate Taylor & the Hessian).
🤖 In ML today

The Hessian is the curvature of the loss landscape. Its eigenvalues classify critical points — and deep-learning losses are dominated by saddle points, not local minima. Curvature drives Newton / quasi-Newton steps, conditioning, and trust regions; second-order Taylor expansions justify Gauss–Newton and Fisher approximations.

🔮 Next

Sharpness-aware minimization (SAM) and the "flat-minima generalize better" line of work; curvature-aware optimizers (Sophia); and influence functions (Hessian-vector products) for data attribution, debugging and machine unlearning.

Block 6

Convexity

📖 Read pp. 76–78 (1-D convex functions), 129–134 (convexity in normed spaces).
🤖 In ML today

A convex problem has a single global minimum and clean duality — so SVMs, logistic/linear regression, lasso and ridge are all convex and come with convergence guarantees. Jensen's inequality underlies the ELBO in variational inference and the EM algorithm.

🔮 Next

Deep nets are non-convex, but convex relaxations power certified robustness and verification, online convex optimization gives bandit/RL regret bounds, and convex duality reappears in optimal transport and in the (near-convex) neural-tangent-kernel regime of very wide networks.

Block 7

Optimization algorithms: GD, SGD, mini-batch

📖 Read pp. 158–165 — gradient descent & stochastic methods (within Taylor's formula & local extrema).
🤖 In ML today

Every model you train is trained this way. Mini-batch SGD is the workhorse; momentum, Adam and AdamW are the deep-learning defaults. Step-size schedules, gradient variance, and convergence rates are daily, practical concerns — not theory footnotes.

🔮 Next

Optimizers for trillion-parameter models — distributed/sharded training (ZeRO), memory-frugal variants (8-bit Adam, Lion), structured second-order methods (Shampoo), and the active theory of why SGD generalizes (implicit regularization).

Block 8

Newton's method · implicit & inverse function theorems

📖 Read pp. 166–172 — Newton's method in $\mathbb{R}^n$, implicit / inverse function theorems.
🤖 In ML today

Newton / quasi-Newton (L-BFGS) solves smaller and convex problems and underlies natural-gradient / K-FAC. The implicit function theorem is how we differentiate through an argmin or a fixed point — the engine of bilevel and hyperparameter optimization and meta-learning.

🔮 Next

Deep equilibrium models (DEQs) and implicit layers solve for a fixed point and backprop via the implicit function theorem — constant-memory, "infinite-depth" networks — alongside differentiable optimization layers (OptNet, cvxpylayers).

Block 9

Constrained extrema · submanifolds & Lagrange multipliers

📖 Read pp. 173–183 — local extrema under constraints, submanifolds, Lagrange multipliers.
🤖 In ML today

Lagrange multipliers / KKT are the theory behind SVM duality, constrained and regularized learning, and the Lagrangian used in constrained/safe RL and fairness constraints. Submanifolds formalize the manifold hypothesis — that real data lives on a low-dimensional surface in a high-dimensional space.

🔮 Next

Safe RL and constrained RLHF via Lagrangian methods; Riemannian optimization on Stiefel/Grassmann manifolds (orthogonality constraints in attention and normalizing flows); and physics-/constraint-informed learning.

Block 10

Operator norms & the (von) Neumann series

📖 Read pp. 101–111 — continuous linear maps, operator norm, Neumann series; Hilbert spaces pp. 112–124.
🤖 In ML today

The spectral (operator) norm of a weight matrix bounds how much a layer stretches its input — so it controls a network's Lipschitz constant, stability and generalization. Spectral normalization stabilizes GAN training and bounds Transformer sensitivity. The Neumann series $(I-A)^{-1}=\sum_k A^k$ is how iterative linear solvers, PageRank, and influence-function / implicit-gradient computations avoid forming an explicit inverse. Hilbert spaces give us kernels / RKHS (SVMs, Gaussian processes) and the function-space view of wide networks.

🔮 Next

Operator learning — Fourier Neural Operators and DeepONets that learn maps between function spaces — is the mathematics of "AI for PDEs / science," and operator-norm control is increasingly how we keep very large Transformers stable at scale.

Bottom line. First-year Analysis is, almost line for line, the language of modern ML: limits and continuity give stability, derivatives give training, Taylor/Hessian give curvature, convexity gives guarantees, and operators give scale. The frontier (implicit layers, neural ODEs, operator learning, geometric deep learning) is built directly on Part II of these notes.
