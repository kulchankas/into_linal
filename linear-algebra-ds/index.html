🎯 Reinforcement
No end-of-chapter exercises — use the mini-lab below instead of skipping practice.
🔬 SVD / PCA mini-lab
  1. By hand ($2\times2$): For $A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$, find singular values and an orthonormal pair of singular vectors. Check with numpy.linalg.svd.
  2. Rank-1 approximation: Take a small grayscale image matrix (e.g. 32×32). Plot original vs. rank-$k$ SVD reconstruction for $k=1,5,20$. Where does most energy live?
  3. PCA pipeline: On a toy dataset (Iris or synthetic 2D clusters): center data → covariance → eigenvectors → project to 2D. Compare to sklearn.decomposition.PCA.
  4. Pseudoinverse: Solve an overdetermined $Ax=b$ via $A^+$ from SVD; match numpy.linalg.lstsq.
  5. Spectral vs SVD: For a symmetric $B$, verify SVD agrees with orthogonal eigendecomposition. When do they differ (non-square $A$)?
  6. Explain Eckart–Young: In one paragraph, why truncating singular values gives the best low-rank approximation in Frobenius norm.
🤖 In data science
The headline result: SVD powers dimensionality reduction (truncated SVD / low-rank approximation via Eckart–Young), recommender systems and latent-semantic analysis, pseudo-inverses for least-squares, and image compression. The spectral theorem guarantees real, orthogonally-diagonalizable covariance matrices — the bedrock of PCA.
🗣 Before moving on
Can you explain: (1) why every matrix has an SVD even when it's not diagonalizable. (2) How singular values relate to $\|A\|_2$ and to PCA. (3) What "normal operator" means and why the spectral theorem needs it.
